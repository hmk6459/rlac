Response 1: Recognizing the Relation between Data and Context


In my initial analysis of the Harry Potter corpus, my partner and I created visualizations to track the "magical infrastructure" across J.K. Rowling’s original novels and several popular fanfictions. 

Figure 1. Word Frequency Heatmap from Assignment 1

Looking at the Word Frequency Heatmap (Figure 3), a clear pattern appeared: while Rowling’s Deathly Hallows is saturated with terms like "wand" and "hogwarts," fanfictions like the Lyingleia_NYU text are almost entirely silent. My immediate hypothesis was that this text was a non-magical story, fundamentally detached from the original corpus. This initial, intuitive reaction is exactly where we can actually go wrong with making conclusions based on first impressions.

Step 1: The AI’s Reasoning Error
To test the "literacy" of LLM interpreters, I provided my heatmap to an LLM without giving it any source context. 

Figure 2.0 LLM Response on Image of Heat Cloud no Context

Figure 3.0 LLM Response on Image of Heat Cloud based on asking Can you explain what you understand and get from this screenshot ?

The result, seen in the First LLM Screenshot of Key Insights, was logical but ultimately not interily correct.
The AI correctly identified the visual "void" but filled in the gaps with the wrong guess. It suggested that the Lyingleia_NYU text was "nearly silent" and might be "very short" or an "academic/analytical piece." This is a classic example of what Maxim Lisnic (2023) describes as a reasoning error. Because the visual lacked contextual "safeguards," the LLM committed an error by over-simplifying the data and inferring that an absence of magical keywords meant an absence of narrative altogether. As Lisnic argues, a human guide makes graph data more meaningful and helps us draw conclusions from it. 

Step 2: Correcting the Big Pattern with the Small Details
Next, I next provided the LLM with a second visual: 

Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions). From Assignment 1

Nexr, I asked it, “Now look at this word cloud. Does this change your earlier guess that the NYU text is 'academic' or 'short'? How do you explain the high frequency of these social words compared to the zero wands/spells we saw in the heatmap?" 

Figure 5. LLM response on Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions).


By seeing words like "dorm," "text," and "kiss" alongside character names like "Harry" and "Draco," the LLM pivoted. 
It recognized that the zeros weren't a data error, but a deliberate narrative choice where the setting has shifted to a modern "College AU" (Alternate Universe). This interaction also validates Ted Underwood’s warning in The Risks of Distant Reading, which I also reflected on in assignment 1. The model can identify patterns, but it cannot fully understand the shared cultural "situatedness" of a fanfiction genre until a human provides the proper interpretative bridge.


Finally, I pushed the LLM with a final interrogation. I asked it to look closer at the text, prompting it with, “Does this mean the story isn't fully without magic? Or  a story where magic exists in the background? How does this change your view of the 'zeros' in the Heatmap?”


Figure 6. LLM response to follow up question based on Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions).
 
The Final LLM Response provided the most interesting analysis of this process. The AI admitted that its "No-Magic" interpretation was too simple. It realized that the "zeros" in the heatmap were not truly zero; they were evidence of "relative scarcity." Magic was present, but so normalized in the story that it was just part of the characters' identities, not the plot engine. As a researcher, this taught me that visualizations can act as maps that highlight the "war" (the 580 wands), but hide the subtle background (the 2 mentions of magic). The zeros were a choice, not an omission.

Conclusion: Visual Literacy is Human Literacy
This short experiment fundamentally changed how I view the relationship between my data and my writing. Initially, I treated my charts as "proof" that could speak for themselves. However, when I tested them against an external observer (an LLM), I realized that my visuals were vulnerable to an LLM’s interpretation and needed additional input or details to get the most effective feedback.
