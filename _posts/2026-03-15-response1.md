Response 1: Recognizing the Relation between Data and Context


In my initial analysis of the Harry Potter corpus, my assignment partner and I created visualizations to track the "magical infrastructure" across J.K. Rowling’s original novels and several popular fanfictions. 

![Heatmap](/rlac/assets/images/fig8.png)
Figure 1. Word Frequency Heatmap from Assignment 1

Looking at the Word Frequency Heatmap (Figure 1), a clear pattern appeared: while Rowling’s Deathly Hallows is saturated with terms like "wand" and "hogwarts," fanfictions like the Harry Potter in NYU text, seem to be entirely silent. My immediate hypothesis, which my partner also shared, was that this text was a non-magical story, fundamentally detached from the original corpus. This initial, intuitive reaction is sometimes the very first reason where we can wrong with making conclusions based on first impressions.


To test the "literacy" of LLM interpreters, I first provided my heatmap to an LLM without giving it any source context. 

![LLM R1](/rlac/assets/images/fig9.png)
Figure 2 LLM Response on Image of Heat Cloud (No context given)

The LLM seemed to do a great job at recreating the heatmap that was given with some colorful visuals, and the LLM’s ability to "see" and recreate the data was impressive. It also gave back the data in terms of numbers, and made initial suggestions. Next, I began asking it question. 


![LLM R2](/rlac/assets/images/fig10.png)
Figure 3. LLM Response on Heatmap Figure, based on the prompt, "Can you explain what you understand and get from this screenshot?" 

The result, seen in the Second LLM Screenshot of Key Insights, was logical but ultimately not interily correct. The AI correctly identified the visual "void" but filled in the gaps with the wrong guess. It suggested that the Lyingleia_NYU text was "nearly silent" and might be "very short" or an "academic/analytical piece." This is a classic example of what Maxim Lisnic (2023) describes as a reasoning error. Because the visual lacked contextual "safeguards," the LLM committed an error by over-simplifying the data and inferring that an absence of magical keywords meant an absence of narrative altogether. As Lisnic argues, a human guide makes graph data more meaningful and helps us draw conclusions from it. 



Followingly, I next provided the LLM with a second visual: 

![LLM R3](/rlac/assets/images/fig11.png)
Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions) From Assignment 1

Next, I asked it, “Now look at this word cloud. Does this change your earlier guess that the NYU text is 'academic' or 'short'? How do you explain the high frequency of these social words compared to the zero wands/spells we saw in the heatmap?" 

![LLM R4](/rlac/assets/images/fig12.png)
Figure 5. LLM response on Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions)


By seeing words like "dorm," "text," and "kiss" alongside character names like "Harry" and "Draco," the LLM seemed to have made its reflections known. It recognized the zeros on the first figure, and described it as a deliberate narrative choice where the setting has shifted to a modern "College AU" (Alternate Universe). This interaction also validates Ted Underwood’s warning in The Risks of Distant Reading, which I also reflected on in assignment 1. The model can identify patterns, but it may not fully understand the shared cultural of a fanfiction genre until a human provides the proper interpretative connection.


Finally, I pushed the LLM with a final interrogation. I asked it to look closer at the text, prompting it with, “Does this mean the story isn't fully without magic? Or  a story where magic exists in the background? How does this change your view of the 'zeros' in the Heatmap?”


![LLM R5](/rlac/assets/images/fig13.png)
Figure 6. LLM response to follow up question based on Figure 4 (The Word Cloud of Fanfictions)
 
The Final LLM Response provided the most interesting analysis of this process. The AI admitted that its "No-Magic" interpretation was too simple. It realized that the "zeros" in the heatmap did not fully mean no magic was present. Instead, it reflects that Magic might have indeed been present, but so normalized in the story that it was just part of the characters' identities, not the plot engine. Ending Reflection:
This short experiment fundamentally changed how I view the relationship between my data and my writing. Initially, I treated my charts as "proof" that could speak for themselves. However, when I tested them against an external observer (an LLM), I realized that I could benefit from far greater feedback based on the LLM’s interpretation, if given additional input or details.
